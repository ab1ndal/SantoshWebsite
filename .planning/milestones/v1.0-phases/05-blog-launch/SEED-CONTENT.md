**Title:** What is Group II+ Base Oil and Why India's Lubricant Industry is Shifting to It
**Slug:** what-is-group-ii-plus-base-oil
**Tag:** Technical
**Read Time:** 5

### Article Body

India's lubricant industry is undergoing a quiet but consequential technical upgrade. Across blending facilities from Ghaziabad to Chennai, procurement engineers are asking a question that barely registered a decade ago: is this a Group I or a Group II+ base oil?

The answer now has regulatory and commercial consequences. Here is what the shift means and why it matters for India's lubricant supply chain.

## Understanding the Base Oil Groups

The American Petroleum Institute (API) classifies base oils into five groups based on composition and performance parameters.

**Group I** base oils — the traditional workhorse of India's blending industry — are produced by solvent refining. They carry relatively high sulfur content (above 300ppm) and a Viscosity Index (VI) of 80–95. IFP Petro Products' SAMSARA brand, a long-standing Indian re-refined base oil (RRBO), falls into this category through its adsorption-based process.

**Group II** base oils are produced through hydroprocessing. Sulfur drops below 300ppm and VI reaches 80–120. These oils demonstrate better oxidation stability, extending lubricant service intervals — an increasingly important factor for modern engine specifications.

**Group II+** is an informal but widely used sub-category of Group II. The defining markers: Viscosity Index at or above 95, sulfur well below 300ppm, and flash point above 200°C. Operationally, Group II+ base oils provide the performance foundation for BS-VI-compliant engine oils.

**Group III** base oils approach synthetic performance through severe hydroprocessing, with VI above 120.

## Why Group II+ Is Now the Minimum Bar for BS-VI Lubricants

India adopted BS-VI emission standards in April 2020 — the equivalent of Euro VI, skipping the BS-V step entirely. The jump was intentional and significant. BS-VI engines run at higher operating temperatures, tighter tolerances, and require lubricants that provide sustained protection under these conditions.

The problem with Group I base stocks for BS-VI formulations is structural. Adsorption-based re-refining removes color and odor but cannot remove sulfur or saturate aromatic structures. The resulting base oil — while useful for many applications — lacks the oxidation stability and deposit-control performance that BS-VI PCMO and HDO (heavy-duty diesel oil) specifications demand.

Major Original Equipment Manufacturers including Maruti, Hyundai, Tata Motors, and Mahindra have progressively updated their factory-fill and service specifications. A growing number now require Group II+ as the minimum base stock for approved lubricants. This is not a preference — it is a technical requirement tied to warranty compliance.

## The Re-Refining Angle: Circular Economy at Group II+ Quality

India generates approximately 1.3 million metric tonnes of used lubricating oil annually. Fewer than 15% of that volume is formally recycled through authorized channels. The vast majority is either burned as low-grade industrial fuel, diluted into diesel substitutes, or dumped — all of which destroy the resource value and create serious environmental liability.

Modern vacuum distillation combined with catalytic hydrotreating changes this equation. Instead of adsorption — which polishes the oil's surface properties without transforming its molecular structure — hydrotreating reacts sulfur compounds with hydrogen under pressure in the presence of metallic catalysts. Sulfur leaves the process as hydrogen sulfide gas. Aromatic structures are saturated into paraffins. The result is a base oil with Group II+ properties derived entirely from recovered feedstock.

This is the process Santosh Petrochemical Innovations has installed at its Ghaziabad facility, using technology developed in collaboration with Indian Oil Corporation Limited. The process delivers VI ≥95, sulfur below 300ppm, and flash point exceeding 200°C — matching Group II virgin base oil performance from what was otherwise a waste stream.

The India waste oil recycling market is valued at approximately USD 3.38 billion in 2025 and growing at ~7.9% CAGR. EPR mandates require lubricant manufacturers to channel increasing percentages of used oil through registered recyclers — 5% in FY2025 scaling to 50% by FY2031.

### Article Body

Extended Producer Responsibility for used lubricating oil is no longer a policy proposal. Since April 1, 2024, it is law under the Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules 2016, as amended in 2023. If your organisation manufactures, imports, or brands lubricants in India, compliance is now mandatory — and the annual targets are climbing.

This guide explains the framework, the timeline, and the practical steps your organisation needs to take.

## What EPR for Used Oil Actually Is

Extended Producer Responsibility is a regulatory model that assigns end-of-life responsibility to the producers who put products into the market. For lubricants, the logic is direct: the companies that manufacture or import lubricants are the entities best placed to ensure that used oil — the spent lubricant that comes out of engines and machinery — does not contaminate the environment.

India classifies used lubricating oil as a hazardous waste under Schedule IV of the Hazardous Waste Rules. Improper disposal is already illegal. EPR adds an additional obligation: a quantified, verified obligation to ensure that a percentage of the used oil corresponding to your sales volume is collected and channelised through authorised recyclers.

The mechanism is market-based. The CPCB (Central Pollution Control Board) has built a digital portal for EPR in the lubricant sector. Lubricant producers register on this portal. Authorised re-refiners — those with valid PCB consent to operate and listed on the CPCB EPR registry — collect used oil and issue EPR certificates. Producers purchase these certificates to demonstrate compliance.

## Who Is Affected

The regulation applies to:

- **Lubricant manufacturers** with manufacturing facilities in India
- **Importers** who bring finished lubricants or lubricant base stocks into India
- **Brand owners** who market lubricants under their brands regardless of where manufacturing occurs

Small producers below a specified annual volume threshold have reduced obligations, but the threshold is low enough that virtually every organised lubricant business is covered.

## The Compliance Timeline

The EPR targets are structured as a progressive ramp, giving the recycling ecosystem time to build capacity:

| Financial Year | RRBO Blending Target |
|---|---|
| FY 2025 (Apr 2024 – Mar 2025) | 5% of used oil against lubricant production |
| FY 2026 | 10% |
| FY 2027 | 15% |
| FY 2028 | 25% |
| FY 2029 | 35% |
| FY 2030 | 40% |
| FY 2031 | 50% |

The word "ramp" understates the scale of the endpoint. At 50% by FY2031, India's lubricant manufacturers will collectively need formal RRBO inputs for half of their production volumes. Current formal recycling sits at under 15% of used oil generated annually. The gap between available formal supply and required demand is the investment thesis driving new capacity, including Santosh Petrochemical's facility.

## What You Need to Do

**Step 1: Register on the CPCB EPR Portal**

Log in at the CPCB hazardous waste EPR portal and create an account as a Lubricant Producer/Importer/Brand Owner. The registration requires your GST number, manufacturing/import records, and contact details.

**Step 2: Calculate Your Annual Obligation**

Your EPR obligation is based on your lubricant sales volume for the prior financial year. The CPCB portal provides calculation guidance. The figure you arrive at is the quantity of used oil (in metric tonnes) that must be demonstrably channelised through authorised recyclers on your behalf.

**Step 3: Procure EPR Certificates**

Purchase EPR certificates from CPCB-registered re-refiners. Certificates are issued per tonne of used oil formally collected and recycled. A re-refiner can only issue certificates against oil that has actually been processed — the system is auditable.

**Step 4: Submit Annual Returns**

File your annual EPR compliance return on the CPCB portal before the due date for each financial year. The return reconciles your production volumes against the EPR certificates you have procured.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

The Hazardous Waste Rules carry penalties under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986. Beyond financial penalties, non-compliant manufacturers face the reputational risk of being listed on the CPCB public portal as a defaulter — increasingly relevant as large institutional buyers include EPR compliance in their supplier qualification criteria.

**Title:** Re-Refined Base Oil vs Virgin Base Oil: A Technical Comparison

### Article Body

The procurement engineer's scepticism about re-refined base oil (RRBO) is understandable. For decades, RRBO carried a justified reputation for inconsistent quality: dark colour, elevated sulfur, reduced oxidation stability. Lubricant blenders who tried it once and burned — literally, through a customer warranty claim — learned to avoid it.

That reputation was earned by a previous generation of re-refining technology. It no longer reflects the state of the art. Here is the technical comparison that matters for 2026 procurement decisions.

## Why the Old Reputation Was Accurate — and Why It No Longer Is

The old process was acid-clay treatment or adsorption-based re-refining. The chemistry:

1. Used oil is heated and filtered to remove particles and water
2. Acid treatment or clay adsorption removes sludge and residual additives
3. The resulting product is filtered and blended for sale

What this process **cannot** do is remove sulfur. It cannot saturate the aromatic hydrocarbon structures that degrade oxidation stability. The output is Group I by nature — sulfur content in the 0.2–1.2% range, VI in the 80–95 band, residual colour often above ASTM 3.

This is the process used by India's most established re-refined base oil producers, including IFP Petro Products (capacity 18,000 MTPA, Sahibabad). Their product works for applications where Group I base stock is acceptable. For BS-VI-compliant lubricant formulations, it is insufficient.

The new process is vacuum distillation plus catalytic hydrotreating — the REVA technology implemented at Santosh Petrochemical's Ghaziabad facility:

1. **Dehydration**: Remove water, glycols, and light ends at ~120°C
2. **Vacuum Distillation (WFE)**: Fractionate the oil at 280–310°C under vacuum, separating base fractions from asphalt residue
3. **Catalytic Hydrotreating**: React with hydrogen under pressure in the presence of metallic catalysts — sulfur leaves as H₂S gas, aromatic rings are saturated into paraffins
4. **QC**: Verify VI, sulfur, flash point, pour point per ASTM standards

The hydrotreating step transforms the molecular structure of the base oil. It does not polish it — it reworks it.

## The IS 18722 Standard: India Sets the Bar

India has done something no other country has done: created a dedicated BIS standard specifically for re-refined base oil. BIS IS 18722 (Part 1) defines quality thresholds for RRBO that are, in several parameters, stricter than Group I virgin base oil standards.

The intent is explicit — IS 18722 was designed to ensure that RRBO sold in India meets a defined, auditable minimum quality that protects lubricant blenders and end users. The standard covers flash point, viscosity index, sulfur content, and oxidative stability requirements.

Santosh Petrochemical's Group II+ output is formulated to meet IS 18722 specifications. This matters for procurement: BIS compliance is becoming a qualification criterion in institutional tenders.

**Slug:** how-to-dispose-used-oil-ncr

### Article Body

If you run a workshop, a fleet operation, or an industrial facility in the Delhi-NCR region, used engine oil is a regular operational output. What you do with it matters more than most people realise — both legally and environmentally.

This guide explains the correct procedure for used oil disposal in NCR and Western UP, the regulations that apply, and how to arrange authorised collection.

## Why Used Oil Disposal Is Not Optional

Used lubricating oil is classified as a hazardous waste under Schedule IV of India's Hazardous and Other Wastes (Management and Transboundary Movement) Rules, 2016. This is a legal classification, not a general caution. The classification means:

- Storage, transport, and disposal are regulated activities
- Improper disposal can result in penalties under the Environment (Protection) Act, 1986
- Facilities generating used oil above threshold quantities must maintain records and dispose through authorised channels

The environmental stakes are correspondingly serious. One litre of used lubricating oil, if poured onto soil or into a drainage channel, can contaminate up to one million litres of groundwater. Used oil is not biodegradable in the conventional sense — it persists in soil and water, suppressing microbial activity and entering the food chain through ground-level contamination.

## What Not to Do

The following disposal practices are not only environmentally harmful but are violations of Indian hazardous waste regulations:

- **Pouring used oil into drains or onto soil**: The most common informal disposal method and the most damaging. Any discharge of hazardous waste to land or water bodies is prohibited.
- **Burning used oil as fuel**: Informal burning of used oil in boilers, furnaces, or brick kilns without a co-processing permit is illegal in most states. Combustion of unrefined used oil releases dioxins, furans, and heavy metals into the atmosphere.
- **Mixing with solid waste**: Used oil must not be mixed with general solid waste. Once mixed, the entire waste stream is potentially classified as hazardous, creating a larger disposal problem.
- **Selling to unregistered buyers**: Selling used oil to buyers who cannot produce their PCB consent-to-operate certificate creates liability for the generator. If the buyer misuses the oil, the original generator's records become relevant to enforcement.

## The Correct Process

**Step 1: Segregate and store properly**

Collect used oil in clearly labelled, sealed containers — steel drums or high-density polyethylene (HDPE) containers are appropriate. Keep used oil away from water, food materials, and other waste streams. Mark containers with "Used Lubricating Oil — Hazardous Waste."

**Step 2: Maintain a waste register**

Generators above the threshold quantity (specified in the Hazardous Waste Rules) must maintain a log of used oil quantities generated, stored, and disposed. This becomes important during regulatory inspections.

**Step 3: Contact an authorised re-refiner or recycler**

Used oil must be channelised to a facility with valid PCB (Pollution Control Board) consent to operate as a re-refiner or recycler of hazardous waste. The authorised facility will collect the oil and issue a Form 6 manifest — the official transfer document under the Hazardous Waste Rules. Keep this document.

**Step 4: Verify the Form 6 manifest**

When the used oil is collected, ensure the collector issues a Form 6 manifest that includes: the authorised facility's name and PCB registration number, the quantity transferred, and the date of transfer. This document is your compliance record.
